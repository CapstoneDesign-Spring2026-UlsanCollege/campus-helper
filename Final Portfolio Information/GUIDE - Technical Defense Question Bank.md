# Technical Defense Question Bank

## Purpose

During the final presentation, the instructor may ask individual students technical-defense questions.

You will not know exactly which questions you will receive.

You are not expected to memorize perfect answers. You are expected to understand your project well enough to answer clearly and honestly.

## How to Prepare

For each question:

1. answer in simple language,
2. point to a file, Issue, PR, test, screenshot, or document when useful,
3. explain what you personally know,
4. say honestly when you do not know,
5. and explain how you would verify the answer.

## A. Project Value and Scope

1. Who is the main user of your project?
2. What problem does your MVP solve?
3. What is the most important user flow in your final MVP?
4. Which feature did your team cut, and why?
5. What is one feature you moved to Nice Later?

## B. Individual Contribution

6. What was your strongest personal contribution?
7. Show one PR, Issue, commit, test, or document that proves your contribution.
8. What problem did you personally help solve?
9. What is one mistake you made during the project, and what did you learn?
10. If you had one more week, what would you improve first?

## C. Architecture and Code Flow

11. Show one file or folder you understand well. What is its purpose?
12. Explain how data moves through one important user flow.
13. Where does user input enter the system?
14. Where is validation handled?
15. What happens after the user clicks one important button?
16. Which component, route, endpoint, or function is most important to the MVP?
17. How does your frontend communicate with your backend or data source?
18. What external services or libraries does your project depend on?

## D. Testing and Reliability

19. How did your team test the core user flow?
20. Show one test case and explain why it matters.
21. What happens when the user enters invalid input?
22. What is one edge case your team checked?
23. Did you use automated tests, manual tests, or both? Why?
24. What is one reliability risk that still exists?

## E. Bugs and Limitations

25. What was the most important bug your team fixed?
26. What bug or limitation still exists?
27. How did your team decide the severity of a bug?
28. What workaround should a user know about?

## F. GitHub Workflow and Evidence

29. Show one GitHub Issue that led to real work.
30. Show one PR and explain what changed.
31. How did your team use branches and reviews?
32. What evidence proves that one feature is actually done?

## G. AI Use and Human Review

33. Which AI tools did your team use?
34. Show one AI-assisted change. What did a human check?
35. How did your team test AI-generated or AI-suggested code?
36. Did AI ever suggest something incorrect, risky, or unnecessary?
37. What part of the project do you still not fully understand?

## H. Deployment and Handoff

38. How can a new developer run the project?
39. What environment variables, accounts, or setup steps are required?
40. If the live demo fails, what is your backup plan?

## Strong Answer Pattern

A strong answer usually includes:

- a direct answer,
- one concrete example,
- one evidence link,
- and an honest limitation when relevant.

## Weak Answer Pattern

Weak answers sound like:

- “AI wrote it.”
- “My teammate knows.”
- “It worked before.”
- “I am not sure where the file is.”
- “We did not test that.”
- “It is almost done.”

If you do not know, say:

> I do not know yet. I would inspect this file, run this test, or ask this teammate to verify it.

That is better than inventing an answer.
